STEPS Technical Appendix

Scalable Training Estimation and Planning System for FETP India. This appendix explains, step by step, how the STEPS decision aid translates preference estimates, cost templates and epidemiological multipliers into endorsement, costs, benefits and national scale up results. Default epidemiological settings assume a five year planning horizon and an exchange rate of 83 INR per USD for display.

The appendix is written as a stand alone technical manual. It is designed so that analysts can reproduce every calculation in Excel, R, Stata or other software, and adapt the approach for Field Epidemiology Training Programmes in other settings, not only for India. All formulas are stated explicitly and every symbol is defined in the text.

1. Overview of the preference study and STEPS

The STEPS tool is built on a discrete choice experiment (DCE) that asked stakeholders to choose between alternative Field Epidemiology Training Program (FETP) designs and an explicit opt out option. Each training option was described by:

  • Programme tier: Frontline, Intermediate or Advanced.
  • Career incentive: government and partner certificate, university qualification, or government career pathway.
  • Mentorship intensity: low, medium or high.
  • Delivery mode: blended, fully in person, or fully online.
  • Expected response time for events: within 30, 15 or 7 days.
  • Cost per trainee per month in Indian rupees.

In each DCE task respondents viewed two FETP options (Training A and Training B) plus an opt out alternative where no new training option was funded. By observing how respondents traded off attributes and cost across many tasks, the study estimated preference parameters that describe how attractive each feature is on average and how this differs across people.

STEPS links three ingredients:

  1. Preference estimates from the DCE (a mixed logit model and a two class latent class model).
  2. Cost templates from WHO, NIE and NCDC for different FETP tiers, including opportunity cost of trainee time where available.
  3. Simple epidemiological multipliers that translate training outputs into indicative public health benefits.

These inputs are combined to produce, for any user selected configuration:

  • Predicted endorsement and opt out rates.
  • Willingness to pay for attribute improvements.
  • Programme cost per cohort and economic cost including opportunity cost.
  • Indicative monetary benefits, net benefit and benefit cost ratio.
  • National scale results when the configuration is rolled out to multiple cohorts.
STEPS is a decision support tool, not a full transmission model. It focuses on transparent, easily explained indicators that can be used in policy discussions and business case documents and it can be aligned with more detailed modelling where needed.

1.1 Purpose of STEPS and link to this appendix

The main STEPS interface (index.html) allows users to select FETP configurations and see results in real time. It is aimed at senior decision makers and technical counterparts in ministries and partner agencies. This appendix provides the detailed technical documentation that underpins those results. Each equation that appears in the code is reproduced here in readable form. Where the app displays a number such as an endorsement rate, a WTP value or a benefit cost ratio, this appendix states the formula used to compute it and explains how each term is obtained from the DCE, cost templates or epidemiological configuration.

1.2 Replication and adaptation beyond India

Although the current implementation of STEPS is calibrated to Indian FETP data and cost templates, the approach is intentionally generic. Any country or institution that has preference data for training configurations, cost templates for programme delivery and basic epidemiological assumptions can adapt the framework. To do this in another setting, analysts need to:

  • Replace the DCE coefficients with estimates from their own stakeholder survey.
  • Update cost templates so that programme and opportunity cost per trainee per month reflect local data.
  • Set epidemiological multipliers and values per graduate and per outbreak response that reflect local priorities.

All subsequent sections describe the calculations using generic notation and emphasise where India specific inputs enter the formulas. This separation between structure and parameters is deliberate, so that the STEPS framework can be reused for other FETP programmes and other types of training.

The STEPS user interface is organised into several tabs:

2. Preference model and utilities

The main model used in STEPS is a mixed logit (MXL) model estimated on the DCE data. The model includes:

  • An alternative specific constant for Training A (ASC_A).
  • An alternative specific constant for the opt out option (ASC_OptOut).
  • Dummy variables for each non cost attribute level relative to a baseline.
  • A linear cost term measured in thousands of rupees per trainee per month.

For individual n, alternative j and task t, the systematic utility in the mixed logit specification is:

U_njt = ASC\_A × I(j = A) + ASC\_OptOut × I(j = OptOut) + Σ_k [ beta_k × x_kjt ] + beta_cost × cost_njt

where:

  • U_njt is the systematic utility of alternative j for individual n in choice task t.
  • I(j = A) is an indicator equal to 1 if the alternative is Training A and 0 otherwise.
  • I(j = OptOut) is 1 for the opt out alternative and 0 otherwise.
  • beta_k is the coefficient associated with attribute level k.
  • x_kjt equals 1 if attribute level k is present in alternative j in task t, and 0 otherwise.
  • beta_cost is the cost coefficient, measured per 1,000 INR per trainee per month.
  • cost_njt is the cost per trainee per month in thousands of INR for alternative j.

In the mixed logit specification the preference parameters are allowed to vary across individuals:

beta_{kn} = beta_k + eta_{kn}

where eta_{kn} is an individual specific deviation, typically assumed to follow a normal distribution with mean zero and variance estimated from the data. In the STEPS decision aid, the utilities used for endorsement and WTP calculations rely on the estimated mean coefficients beta_k. Random variation is used in model estimation but is not simulated in the user interface, to keep the outputs stable and easy to explain.

In the STEPS code the mixed logit mean coefficients (measured at the sample average) are:

Baseline levels have coefficient zero by construction. For example Frontline tier, government and partner certificate, low mentorship, blended delivery and response within 30 days are treated as reference categories.

2.2 Latent class supportive group (Class 2)

In addition to the mixed logit model, STEPS uses parameters from a two class latent class model. Class 2 is interpreted as a supportive stakeholder group. Key Class 2 parameters are:

Parameter Interpretation Value
ASC_A Training A constant 0.098
ASC_OptOut Opt out constant -2.543

In the latent class model, each class c has its own parameter vector beta_{kc} and its own class probability. When the user selects the supportive class in the STEPS interface, endorsement and WTP calculations are based on these Class 2 coefficients instead of the mixed logit means.

2.3 Building utility from attribute levels

In the STEPS code, the systematic part of utility for a programme configuration is constructed in two steps:

  1. Compute a non cost component that adds up the constant and all non cost attribute coefficients.
  2. Add the cost term, which is the cost coefficient multiplied by cost per trainee per month in thousands.

The mixed logit non cost utility is:

nonCostUtility = ASC\_A + tier coefficient + career incentive coefficient + mentorship coefficient + delivery mode coefficient + response time coefficient

The cost term is:

costUtility = beta\_cost × (cost per trainee per month in INR / 1,000)

Total utility for the FETP programme relative to Training B is:

V\_FETP = nonCostUtility + costUtility
Example 1: Non cost utility for an Advanced, high mentorship programme

Consider an Advanced FETP configuration with:

  • Programme tier: Advanced.
  • Career incentive: university qualification.
  • Mentorship intensity: high.
  • Delivery mode: blended (reference).
  • Expected response time: within 7 days.

Using the mixed logit means, the non cost utility is:

nonCostUtility = ASC\_A + Advanced + UniQual + MentorHigh + Blended (0) + Resp7 = 0.168 + 0.487 + 0.017 + 0.640 + 0 + 0.610 = 1.922

The same approach is used in the STEPS code for any combination of tier, incentive, mentorship, delivery and response time selected in the Configuration tab.

Example 1b: Comparing Intermediate and Advanced non cost utility

For illustration, compare two configurations that are identical except for programme tier:

  • Configuration X: Intermediate, blended, medium mentorship, response within 15 days.
  • Configuration Y: Advanced, blended, medium mentorship, response within 15 days.

Ignoring cost and career incentives, non cost utility for X is:

nonCostUtility\_X = ASC\_A + Intermediate + MentorMedium + Resp15 = 0.168 + 0.220 + 0.453 + 0.546 = 1.387

For Y (Advanced instead of Intermediate):

nonCostUtility\_Y = 0.168 + 0.487 + 0.453 + 0.546 = 1.654

Advanced has higher non cost utility by 0.267. For the same cost per trainee per month, the model therefore predicts higher endorsement for the Advanced configuration.

2.4 Note on log normal cost specifications

Some DCE applications use a log normal cost distribution to ensure that cost coefficients are always negative. In such a specification the individual specific cost coefficient can be written as:

beta\_{cost,n} = - exp( mu\_cost + sigma\_cost × eta\_n )

where mu_cost and sigma_cost are estimated parameters and eta_n is a standard normal draw. The STEPS decision aid currently uses a linear cost coefficient of -0.005 in the main mixed logit model and -0.001 in the supportive latent class. If future versions adopt a log normal cost specification, the same endorsement and WTP formulas apply, but with the individual specific cost coefficient beta_{cost,n} substituted in place of the fixed value.

3. Endorsement probabilities

STEPS reports endorsement as the predicted share of stakeholders who would support the chosen programme configuration instead of choosing the opt out alternative. To compute this, STEPS compares the utility of the FETP configuration with the utility of opting out.

Let V_FETP denote the total utility of the configured option relative to Training B, and ASC_OptOut denote the opt out constant. The difference in utility between FETP and opt out is:

deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + nonCostUtility + costUtility = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP

With a single FETP option and an opt out, the endorsement probability is:

P(endorse) = exp(deltaV) / (1 + exp(deltaV)) P(opt out) = 1 - P(endorse)
Example 2: Endorsement for the Advanced high mentorship configuration

Continue the configuration from Example 1 and assume a cost per trainee per month of 75,000 INR. The cost term is:

cost per trainee per month = 75,000 INR cost in thousands = 75,000 / 1,000 = 75 cost coefficient = -0.005 costUtility = -0.005 × 75 = -0.375

Total utility for FETP is:

V\_FETP = nonCostUtility + costUtility = 1.922 - 0.375 = 1.547

The opt out constant is ASC_OptOut = -0.601, so the utility difference between FETP and opt out is:

deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP = -(-0.601) + 1.547 = 0.601 + 1.547 = 2.148

The endorsement probability is:

P(endorse) = exp(2.148) / (1 + exp(2.148)) ≈ 8.57 / 9.57 ≈ 0.895 or 89.5 percent

STEPS would display this as roughly 90 percent endorsement and 10 percent opt out. If the cost slider is moved upward, the costUtility term becomes more negative and endorsement falls. If cost is reduced, endorsement increases.

Example 2b: Effect of increasing cost on endorsement

For the same Advanced configuration, increase cost per trainee per month to 250,000 INR:

cost in thousands = 250,000 / 1,000 = 250 costUtility = -0.005 × 250 = -1.25 V\_FETP = 1.922 - 1.25 = 0.672 deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP = 0.601 + 0.672 = 1.273 P(endorse) = exp(1.273) / (1 + exp(1.273)) ≈ 3.57 / 4.57 ≈ 0.78 (78 percent)

Moving the cost slider from 75,000 to 250,000 INR reduces endorsement from around 90 percent to around 78 percent in this example. The Results tab in STEPS reflects exactly this logic.

Example 2c: Frontline configuration with baseline attributes

Consider a Frontline configuration with all reference levels:

  • Tier: Frontline (reference).
  • Career incentive: government and partner certificate (reference).
  • Mentorship: low (reference).
  • Delivery mode: blended (reference).
  • Response time: within 30 days (reference).
  • Cost per trainee per month: 100,000 INR.

Dummy variables are all zero except the Training A indicator. Non cost utility is:

nonCostUtility = ASC\_A = 0.168

The cost term is:

cost in thousands = 100,000 / 1,000 = 100 costUtility = -0.005 × 100 = -0.50 V\_FETP = 0.168 - 0.50 = -0.332

The utility difference between FETP and opt out is:

deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP = 0.601 - 0.332 = 0.269

The endorsement probability is:

P(endorse) = exp(0.269) / (1 + exp(0.269)) ≈ 1.31 / 2.31 ≈ 0.57 or 57 percent

This example illustrates that even a basic Frontline configuration can attract majority support at moderate cost levels.

Example 2d: Intermediate configuration with medium mentorship

Consider an Intermediate configuration with:

  • Tier: Intermediate.
  • Career incentive: university qualification.
  • Mentorship: medium.
  • Delivery mode: blended.
  • Response time: within 15 days.
  • Cost per trainee per month: 200,000 INR.

Non cost utility is:

nonCostUtility = ASC\_A + Intermediate + UniQual + MentorMedium + Resp15 = 0.168 + 0.220 + 0.017 + 0.453 + 0.546 = 1.404

The cost term is:

cost in thousands = 200,000 / 1,000 = 200 costUtility = -0.005 × 200 = -1.00 V\_FETP = 1.404 - 1.00 = 0.404

The utility difference and endorsement are:

deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP = 0.601 + 0.404 = 1.005 P(endorse) = exp(1.005) / (1 + exp(1.005)) ≈ 2.73 / 3.73 ≈ 0.73 or 73 percent

Intermediate options with stronger mentorship and faster response times can achieve higher endorsement even at higher cost per trainee per month.

STEPS applies exactly the same logic when the supportive latent class group is selected, but substitutes the Class 2 parameters in place of the mixed logit means. This allows users to explore endorsement among a more supportive audience and to construct sensitivity analyses based on different stakeholder groups.

4. Willingness to pay (WTP) and DCE benefit construction

Willingness to pay expresses each attribute coefficient in rupee terms per trainee per month. For a DCE with a linear cost term, the marginal willingness to pay for attribute level k is:

WTP\_k = - beta\_k / beta\_cost

Here beta_k is the coefficient on the attribute level and beta_cost is the cost coefficient measured per thousand INR. To convert into rupees per trainee per month, STEPS multiplies by 1,000. WTP is interpreted as the monetary amount per trainee per month that stakeholders are willing to give up in cost in order to obtain that attribute change, holding other attributes constant.

4.1 WTP from the mixed logit model

With a cost coefficient of -0.005, the mixed logit implies the approximate WTP values in the table below.

Attribute level Coefficient WTP (thousand INR) WTP (INR per trainee per month)
Intermediate programme 0.220 44.0 44,000
Advanced programme 0.487 97.4 97,400
Medium mentorship 0.453 90.6 90,600
High mentorship 0.640 128.0 128,000
Fully in person (relative to blended) -0.232 -46.4 -46,400
Fully online (relative to blended) -1.073 -214.6 -214,600
Response within 15 days 0.546 109.2 109,200
Response within 7 days 0.610 122.0 122,000
Example 3: WTP for high mentorship

The coefficient for high mentorship is 0.640. With a cost coefficient of -0.005 the WTP is:

WTP\_highMentor = - 0.640 / (-0.005) = 128 (thousand INR) = 128,000 INR per trainee per month

This means that, averaged across respondents, high mentorship is valued as much as an increase of about 128,000 INR per trainee per month in programme cost. If actual cost is lower than this, high mentorship tends to improve cost effectiveness as long as it also improves epidemiological outcomes.

Example 3b: WTP difference between Intermediate and Advanced tiers

Using the same cost coefficient, the WTP for Intermediate and Advanced relative to Frontline are:

WTP\_Intermediate = -0.220 / (-0.005) = 44 (thousand INR) = 44,000 INR WTP\_Advanced = -0.487 / (-0.005) = 97.4 (thousand INR) ≈ 97,400 INR

Moving from Frontline to Intermediate is valued at around 44,000 INR per trainee per month, while moving from Frontline to Advanced is valued at around 97,400 INR. This gap, combined with different costs and epidemiological multipliers, drives whether Intermediate or Advanced looks more attractive in the STEPS results.

4.2 WTP from the supportive latent class group

For Class 2 of the latent class model the cost coefficient is -0.001. WTP values are therefore larger for a given attribute coefficient. For example, the WTP for an Advanced programme among supportive stakeholders is roughly:

WTP\_Advanced\_Class2 = - 0.422 / (-0.001) ≈ 422 (thousand INR) ≈ 422,000 INR per trainee per month

STEPS uses these WTP values to generate the WTP summary shown in the Results tab. They complement endorsement by indicating the monetary value stakeholders attach to different design features.

WTP is based on stated preferences in a survey context and should not be interpreted as a literal budget willingness to pay by any single organisation. It is best used as a relative measure when comparing design options and prioritising attributes.

4.3 Total WTP per configuration

For any configuration, STEPS can construct a total WTP per trainee per month by summing the WTP associated with each attribute level that is active in that configuration. Let:

  • K be the set of attribute levels that are switched on in the configuration.
  • beta_k be the coefficient for level k ∈ K.
  • beta_cost be the cost coefficient.

The total WTP in thousand INR per trainee per month is:

WTP\_total\_config\_kINR = Σ_{k ∈ K} ( - beta\_k / beta\_cost )

and in INR per trainee per month:

WTP\_total\_config\_INR = 1,000 × WTP\_total\_config\_kINR

This total represents the value stakeholders place on moving from the baseline configuration to the selected configuration, holding cost measured in INR per trainee per month as the numeraire.

Example 4: Total WTP for an illustrative configuration

Consider an illustrative configuration with the following attribute coefficients (for clarity, these values are simplified and do not exactly match the mixed logit table above):

  • beta_tier=Intermediate = 0.40
  • beta_mentorship=High = 0.30
  • beta_delivery=Hybrid = 0.20
  • beta_Resp=15days = 0.546
  • beta_cost = -0.005 per 1,000 INR per trainee per month.

WTP per trainee per month in thousand INR for each attribute is:

WTP\_tier = - 0.40 / (-0.005) = 80 (thousand INR) WTP\_ment = - 0.30 / (-0.005) = 60 (thousand INR) WTP\_delivery = - 0.20 / (-0.005) = 40 (thousand INR) WTP\_Resp15 = - 0.546 / (-0.005) = 109.2 (thousand INR)

Total WTP per trainee per month in thousand INR is:

WTP\_total\_config\_kINR = 80 + 60 + 40 + 109.2 = 289.2

Expressed in INR per trainee per month:

WTP\_total\_config\_INR = 289.2 × 1,000 = 289,200 INR

If the cohort size is 100 trainees and the programme lasts 12 months, the WTP based benefit per cohort is:

Benefit\_WTP\_cohort = 289,200 × 100 × 12 = 28,920,000 × 12 = 347,040,000 INR

If three such cohorts are run within the planning horizon, the total WTP based benefit is:

Benefit\_WTP\_national ≈ 3 × 347,040,000 = 1,041,120,000 INR

These values can be used as DCE based benefit measures in cost benefit analysis, as described later in Sections 7 and 10.

4.4 WTP for outbreak response time

Outbreak response time is a key attribute in the DCE and can be valued directly using WTP. With the mixed logit coefficients:

  • beta_Resp15 = 0.546
  • beta_Resp7 = 0.610
  • beta_cost = -0.005

The WTP per trainee per month in thousand INR for faster response is:

WTP\_Resp15 vs 30 = - 0.546 / (-0.005) = 109.2 (thousand INR) WTP\_Resp7 vs 30 = - 0.610 / (-0.005) = 122.0 (thousand INR)

In INR per trainee per month:

WTP\_Resp15 vs 30 = 109,200 INR WTP\_Resp7 vs 30 = 122,000 INR
Example 4b: Response time WTP per cohort

Consider a Frontline style cohort with:

  • Cohort size: 100 trainees.
  • Programme duration: 3 months.

The WTP per cohort for improving response time from 30 days to 15 days is:

Benefit\_WTP\_Resp15\_cohort = 109,200 × 100 × 3 = 32,760,000 INR

For improving from 30 days to 7 days:

Benefit\_WTP\_Resp7\_cohort = 122,000 × 100 × 3 = 36,600,000 INR

In one cohort, moving from 30 to 15 days is worth about 32.8 million INR in WTP terms, and moving from 30 to 7 days is worth about 36.6 million INR. These values can be compared directly with the incremental cost of investing in faster surveillance and response capacity.

4.5 Effective WTP benefit adjusted for endorsement

WTP based benefits capture the value of programme improvements but do not by themselves account for the fact that some stakeholders may oppose a configuration. For a given scenario s:

  • Benefit_WTP_s is the total WTP based benefit per cohort (for example from Example 4).
  • endorsement_s is the endorsement rate between 0 and 1.

An endorsement weighted or effective WTP benefit can be defined as:

Benefit\_WTP\_effective\_s = Benefit\_WTP\_s × endorsement\_s

In the planned Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab, this effective benefit will be reported alongside the unadjusted WTP based benefit, so that users can see how much of the potential benefit is realised when endorsement is below 100 percent. This is particularly useful when comparing configurations that differ substantially in both endorsement and implied WTP.

5. Programme and economic costs

STEPS builds programme and economic costs at two levels:

  • Per cohort, based on cost per trainee per month chosen in the Configuration tab and programme duration.
  • Across cohorts, based on the number of cohorts entered by the user.

5.1 Programme cost per cohort

For a given configuration, programme cost per cohort (excluding opportunity cost) is:

C\_prog = costPerTraineePerMonth × traineesPerCohort × durationMonths
Example 5: Programme cost per Advanced cohort

Suppose the configuration is:

  • Programme tier: Advanced (24 months).
  • Cohort size: 20 trainees.
  • Cost per trainee per month: 250,000 INR.

Then:

C\_prog = 250,000 × 20 × 24 = 250,000 × 480 = 120,000,000 INR
Example 5b: Programme cost for an Intermediate cohort

Consider an Intermediate configuration with:

  • Programme tier: Intermediate (12 months).
  • Cohort size: 30 trainees.
  • Cost per trainee per month: 150,000 INR.

Programme cost per cohort is:

C\_prog = 150,000 × 30 × 12 = 150,000 × 360 = 54,000,000 INR

Users can compare this with the Advanced example to see how moving from Intermediate to Advanced changes both cost and the epidemiological benefits described in the next section.

5.2 Economic cost and opportunity cost

Cost templates from WHO, NIE and NCDC identify:

  • Shares of cost allocated to staff, travel, materials, supervision and overheads.
  • Where applicable, an opportunity cost component for trainee time.

When the opportunity cost toggle is off, STEPS reports:

  • Programme cost per cohort based on the slider and cohort parameters.
  • Allocation of this cost across components using the chosen template shares.

When the opportunity cost toggle is on, STEPS:

  • Adds the template specific opportunity cost per cohort to programme cost.
  • Reports total economic cost as the sum of programme and opportunity cost.

Economic cost per cohort is:

C\_economic = C\_prog + C\_opp
Example 5c: Economic cost with opportunity cost

Take the Advanced programme example with C_prog = 120,000,000 INR. Assume the cost template indicates that opportunity cost of trainee time is equivalent to 20 percent of programme cost:

C\_opp = 0.20 × 120,000,000 = 24,000,000 INR C\_economic = 120,000,000 + 24,000,000 = 144,000,000 INR

STEPS will show 144,000,000 INR as total economic cost per cohort when opportunity cost is included.

5.3 Cost components

Component specific amounts are obtained by multiplying template shares by the programme cost per cohort. For example, if staff are 40 percent of programme cost, then:

staffCost = 0.40 × C\_prog

Similar calculations are carried out for travel, materials, supervision and overhead categories. These are reported in the Costing details tab and updated each time the user changes cost per trainee, cohort size, programme tier or template source.

5.4 Structure of STEPS cost templates

The STEPS tool reads cost templates for each tier and institution. In the current version:

Template ID Tier Institution Notes
WHO_FL6 Frontline WHO Template for six Frontline cohorts, used to derive per cohort and per trainee per month costs.
WHO_INT Intermediate WHO Intermediate cost structure including staff, travel, materials, supervision and overheads.
NIE_INT Intermediate NIE Alternative Intermediate template reflecting NIE delivery arrangements.
NCDC_INT Intermediate NCDC Intermediate template with NCDC specific cost structure.
NIE_ADV Advanced NIE Advanced cost template used for Advanced tier options.
NCDC_ADV Advanced NCDC Advanced cost template with NCDC assumptions.

In the implementation, each template contains:

  • A total cost per cohort consistent with the duration and cohort size used to construct the template.
  • Shares for each cost component (for example staff, travel, supervision, materials, overheads).
  • An explicit line for opportunity cost of trainee time where relevant.

When a user selects a template in the Costing tab, STEPS rescales the shares to match the chosen cost per trainee per month, while preserving the component mix implied by the template.

Example 5d: Applying a WHO Intermediate template

Suppose the WHO Intermediate template implies the following shares of programme cost:

  • Staff: 45 percent.
  • Travel and per diem: 25 percent.
  • Materials and supplies: 10 percent.
  • Supervision and mentoring: 15 percent.
  • Overheads and other: 5 percent.

If the user sets C_prog = 54,000,000 INR (as in Example 5b), STEPS reports:

staffCost = 0.45 × 54,000,000 = 24,300,000 INR travelCost = 0.25 × 54,000,000 = 13,500,000 INR materialsCost = 0.10 × 54,000,000 = 5,400,000 INR supervisionCost = 0.15 × 54,000,000 = 8,100,000 INR overheadCost = 0.05 × 54,000,000 = 2,700,000 INR

These amounts appear in the Costing details table, helping users understand which inputs drive overall costs for a given configuration.

6. Epidemiological multipliers and benefits

STEPS uses simple multipliers to translate FETP training into public health outcomes. These multipliers are specific to each tier:

  • Graduates per cohort as a share of trainees.
  • Outbreak responses per cohort per year.
  • Indicative monetary value per graduate (INR).
  • Indicative monetary value per outbreak response (INR).

Default values are defined in the epidemiological configuration file epi_config.json and can be adjusted in the Advanced settings tab.

6.1 Default epidemiological configuration

In the current version, default values are:

Tier Duration (months) Graduation rate Outbreaks per cohort per year Value per graduate (INR) Value per outbreak response (INR)
Frontline 3 0.90 0.30 400,000 30,000,000
Intermediate 12 0.90 0.50 500,000 30,000,000
Advanced 24 0.95 0.80 600,000 40,000,000

The general section of the configuration also specifies:

  • Planning horizon for outbreak related benefits: 5 years.
  • Display exchange rate: 83 INR per USD (used only for USD labels in the interface).

6.2 Graduates and outbreak responses

Let:

  • gradRate_tier be the graduation rate (share) for the tier.
  • outbreaks_tier be outbreak responses per cohort per year.
  • endorse be the endorsement probability for the configuration.

For one cohort the expected number of graduates is:

graduates\_per\_cohort = traineesPerCohort × gradRate\_tier × endorse

Expected outbreak responses supported per year are:

outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort = outbreaks\_tier × endorse

6.3 Monetary benefits

Let:

  • valueGrad_tier be the indicative value per graduate (INR).
  • valueOutbreak_tier be the indicative value per outbreak response (INR).
  • planningHorizonYears be the number of years benefits are counted for outbreak responses.

Then benefits per cohort are:

benefit\_graduates = graduates\_per\_cohort × valueGrad\_tier benefit\_outbreaks = outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort × valueOutbreak\_tier × planningHorizonYears totalBenefit\_per\_cohort = benefit\_graduates + benefit\_outbreaks
Example 6: Benefits for an Advanced programme

Assume:

  • Advanced tier graduation rate: 0.95.
  • Outbreak responses per cohort per year: 0.8.
  • Planning horizon: 5 years.
  • Value per graduate: 600,000 INR.
  • Value per outbreak response: 40,000,000 INR.
  • Cohort size: 20 trainees.
  • Endorsement: 0.90 (90 percent).

Then expected graduates per cohort are:

graduates\_per\_cohort = 20 × 0.95 × 0.90 = 20 × 0.855 = 17.1 graduates

Graduate related benefits are:

benefit\_graduates = 17.1 × 600,000 = 10,260,000 INR

Outbreak responses per year are:

outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort = 0.8 × 0.90 = 0.72

Over the five year planning horizon:

benefit\_outbreaks = 0.72 × 40,000,000 × 5 = 0.72 × 200,000,000 = 144,000,000 INR

Total indicative benefit per cohort is:

totalBenefit\_per\_cohort = 10,260,000 + 144,000,000 = 154,260,000 INR
Example 6b: Intermediate programme benefits

For an Intermediate configuration with:

  • Graduation rate: 0.90.
  • Outbreak responses per cohort per year: 0.5.
  • Value per graduate: 500,000 INR.
  • Value per outbreak response: 30,000,000 INR.
  • Cohort size: 30 trainees.
  • Endorsement: 0.80 (80 percent).
  • Planning horizon: 5 years.

Graduates per cohort:

graduates\_per\_cohort = 30 × 0.90 × 0.80 = 30 × 0.72 = 21.6 graduates

Graduate benefits:

benefit\_graduates = 21.6 × 500,000 = 10,800,000 INR

Outbreak responses per year:

outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort = 0.5 × 0.80 = 0.40

Outbreak benefits over 5 years:

benefit\_outbreaks = 0.40 × 30,000,000 × 5 = 0.40 × 150,000,000 = 60,000,000 INR

Total benefit per Intermediate cohort:

totalBenefit\_per\_cohort = 10,800,000 + 60,000,000 = 70,800,000 INR

Users can compare this with the Advanced example to see how higher values per graduate and per outbreak response influence benefits as tier increases.

6.4 Response time multipliers and epidemiological uplift

In addition to DCE based WTP for faster outbreak response, STEPS can treat response time as a modifier of epidemiological benefits. Let:

  • o be the expected number of outbreak responses per cohort per year in the base case.
  • V_outbreak be the value per outbreak response (INR).
  • T be the planning horizon in years.

If the base case uses a 30 day response time, the base outbreak benefit per cohort is:

B\_outbreak,30d = o × V\_outbreak × T

Introduce response time multipliers:

  • m_30 = 1.0 for 30 days (reference).
  • m_15 for 15 days, for example 1.2.
  • m_7 for 7 days, for example 1.5.

Then outbreak related benefits for faster response are:

B\_outbreak,15d = B\_outbreak,30d × m\_15 B\_outbreak,7d = B\_outbreak,30d × m\_7
Example 6c: Response time multipliers for Intermediate tier

Assume for an Intermediate configuration:

  • Outbreaks per cohort per year: o = 0.5.
  • Value per outbreak response: V_outbreak = 30,000,000 INR.
  • Planning horizon: T = 5 years.
  • Multipliers: m_30 = 1.0, m_15 = 1.2, m_7 = 1.5.

Base outbreak benefit with 30 day response is:

B\_outbreak,30d = 0.5 × 30,000,000 × 5 = 0.5 × 150,000,000 = 75,000,000 INR

With 15 day response:

B\_outbreak,15d = 75,000,000 × 1.2 = 90,000,000 INR

With 7 day response:

B\_outbreak,7d = 75,000,000 × 1.5 = 112,500,000 INR

The incremental epidemiological benefit of moving from 30 to 15 days is 15,000,000 INR over the planning horizon. For moving from 30 to 7 days it is 37,500,000 INR. These values can be compared with the WTP based response benefits in Example 4b to cross check whether epidemiological and preference based valuations are broadly aligned.

7. National simulation and cost benefit metrics

The National simulation tab scales up cohort level results to the national level using the number of cohorts specified in the Configuration tab. The scaling is direct and transparent.

7.1 Costs and benefits at national level

Let:

  • C_economic be economic cost per cohort.
  • benefit_per_cohort be total indicative benefit per cohort based on epidemiological multipliers.
  • N_cohorts be the number of cohorts.

Then:

totalCost\_national = C\_economic × N\_cohorts totalBenefit\_national = benefit\_per\_cohort × N\_cohorts netBenefit\_national = totalBenefit\_national - totalCost\_national

The national benefit cost ratio is:

BCR\_national = totalBenefit\_national / totalCost\_national

This ratio is the same as the per cohort ratio because the scaling factor N_cohorts cancels out.

7.2 Epidemiological metrics at national level

National level graduates and outbreak responses are:

graduates\_national = graduates\_per\_cohort × N\_cohorts outbreaks\_national = outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort × N\_cohorts

These are reported in the National simulation tab and update whenever the cohort size, number of cohorts, endorsement or epidemiological multipliers are changed.

Example 7: National scaling of the Advanced scenario

Using the Advanced example above:

  • Economic cost per cohort: 144,000,000 INR.
  • Benefit per cohort: 154,260,000 INR.
  • Graduates per cohort: 17.1.
  • Outbreak responses per year per cohort: 0.72.

Suppose the configuration is scaled to 10 cohorts. Then:

totalCost\_national = 144,000,000 × 10 = 1,440,000,000 INR totalBenefit\_national = 154,260,000 × 10 = 1,542,600,000 INR netBenefit\_national = 1,542,600,000 - 1,440,000,000 = 102,600,000 INR

The benefit cost ratio is:

BCR\_national = 1,542,600,000 / 1,440,000,000 ≈ 1.07

National graduates and outbreak responses are:

graduates\_national = 17.1 × 10 = 171 graduates outbreaks\_national = 0.72 × 10 = 7.2 outbreak responses per year
Example 7b: National picture for Intermediate scenario

Using the Intermediate example (Example 6b) and assuming:

  • Economic cost per cohort: 60,000,000 INR (programme plus opportunity cost).
  • Benefit per cohort: 70,800,000 INR.
  • Graduates per cohort: 21.6.
  • Outbreak responses per year per cohort: 0.40.
  • Number of cohorts: 15.

National level results are:

totalCost\_national = 60,000,000 × 15 = 900,000,000 INR totalBenefit\_national = 70,800,000 × 15 = 1,062,000,000 INR netBenefit\_national = 1,062,000,000 - 900,000,000 = 162,000,000 INR BCR\_national = 1,062,000,000 / 900,000,000 ≈ 1.18

Graduates and outbreak responses:

graduates\_national = 21.6 × 15 = 324 graduates outbreaks\_national = 0.40 × 15 = 6 outbreak responses per year

These summaries are what appear in the National simulation tab when the user selects matching cohort numbers and cost assumptions.

7.3 DCE based and combined cost benefit analysis

The examples above use epidemiological benefits only. In the premium version of STEPS, DCE based WTP benefits can be combined with epidemiological benefits to construct several cost benefit metrics. For a scenario s:

  • C_s is economic cost per cohort (INR).
  • B_s_epi is epidemiological benefit per cohort (INR), for example totalBenefit_per_cohort.
  • B_s_WTP is WTP based benefit per cohort (INR), for example from Section 4.3.

A pure DCE based cost benefit analysis treats B_s_WTP as the only benefit:

NPV\_s,DCE = B\_s\_WTP - C\_s BCR\_s,DCE = B\_s\_WTP / C\_s

A combined DCE plus epidemiological CBA uses:

B\_s,total = B\_s\_WTP + B\_s\_epi NPV\_s,total = B\_s,total - C\_s BCR\_s,total = B\_s,total / C\_s

In both cases, decision rules are:

  • The configuration passes a WTP based economic test if BCR_s,DCE > 1 and NPV_s,DCE > 0.
  • It passes a combined test if BCR_s,total > 1 and NPV_s,total > 0.

7.4 Incremental comparisons between scenarios

When comparing two scenarios, indexed 1 and 2, incremental cost benefit metrics can be defined. For a given benefit definition (for example DCE based or combined DCE plus epidemiological):

  • B_1, C_1 are benefit and cost for scenario 1.
  • B_2, C_2 are benefit and cost for scenario 2.

Incremental net present value is:

ΔNPV = (B\_2 - C\_2) - (B\_1 - C\_1) = (B\_2 - B\_1) - (C\_2 - C\_1)

Incremental benefit cost ratio is:

ΔBCR = (B\_2 - B\_1) / (C\_2 - C\_1)

These incremental metrics are useful when ranking a small set of leading options. In the planned Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab, the raw values needed to compute incremental NPV and BCR are made available in a table so that analysts can carry out these comparisons in spreadsheets or other tools.

8. Worked example matching the user interface

This section walks through a complete example that matches a realistic configuration in the STEPS interface. The goal is to show, in one place, how the preference model, costs and epidemiological multipliers interact.

8.1 Configuration

Consider the following configuration, which matches a typical scenario used in workshops:

  • Programme tier: Advanced (24 months).
  • Career incentive: university qualification.
  • Mentorship intensity: high.
  • Delivery mode: blended.
  • Expected response time: within 7 days.
  • Cost per trainee per month: 75,000 INR.
  • Cohort size: 20 trainees.
  • Number of cohorts: 10.
  • Preference model: mixed logit average.
  • Opportunity cost: included.

8.2 Step 1: Utility and endorsement

From Example 1 the non cost utility is 1.922. With cost per trainee per month of 75,000 INR:

costUtility = -0.005 × (75,000 / 1,000) = -0.005 × 75 = -0.375 V\_FETP = 1.922 - 0.375 = 1.547

The opt out constant is -0.601, so:

deltaV = -ASC\_OptOut + V\_FETP = 0.601 + 1.547 = 2.148 P(endorse) ≈ 0.895 (89.5 percent) P(opt out) ≈ 0.105 (10.5 percent)

STEPS will therefore show endorsement close to 90 percent for this configuration.

8.3 Step 2: Programme and economic costs

Programme cost per cohort is:

C\_prog = 75,000 × 20 × 24 = 75,000 × 480 = 36,000,000 INR

Suppose the chosen cost template implies an opportunity cost equal to 25 percent of programme cost. Then:

C\_opp = 0.25 × 36,000,000 = 9,000,000 INR C\_economic = 36,000,000 + 9,000,000 = 45,000,000 INR per cohort

8.4 Step 3: Epidemiological multipliers and benefits

Assume:

  • Advanced graduation rate: 0.95.
  • Outbreak responses per cohort per year: 0.8.
  • Value per graduate: 600,000 INR.
  • Value per outbreak response: 40,000,000 INR.
  • Planning horizon: 5 years.

With endorsement of 0.895 and cohort size of 20:

graduates\_per\_cohort = 20 × 0.95 × 0.895 ≈ 17.03 graduates

Graduate benefits are:

benefit\_graduates = 17.03 × 600,000 ≈ 10,218,000 INR

Outbreak responses per year are:

outbreaks\_per\_year\_per\_cohort = 0.8 × 0.895 ≈ 0.716

Over five years:

benefit\_outbreaks ≈ 0.716 × 40,000,000 × 5 ≈ 0.716 × 200,000,000 ≈ 143,200,000 INR

Total indicative benefit per cohort is:

totalBenefit\_per\_cohort ≈ 10,218,000 + 143,200,000 ≈ 153,418,000 INR

8.5 Step 4: Benefit cost ratio and net benefit

Per cohort:

netBenefit\_per\_cohort ≈ 153,418,000 - 45,000,000 ≈ 108,418,000 INR BCR\_per\_cohort ≈ 153,418,000 / 45,000,000 ≈ 3.41

If a DCE based WTP benefit is also computed for this configuration, for example using the approach in Section 4.3, then pure DCE based and combined DCE plus epidemiological benefit cost ratios can be reported separately in the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab.

8.6 Step 5: National scaling

With 10 cohorts:

totalCost\_national = 45,000,000 × 10 = 450,000,000 INR totalBenefit\_national ≈ 153,418,000 × 10 ≈ 1,534,180,000 INR netBenefit\_national ≈ 1,534,180,000 - 450,000,000 ≈ 1,084,180,000 INR

Graduates and outbreak responses are:

graduates\_national ≈ 17.03 × 10 ≈ 170 graduates outbreaks\_national ≈ 0.716 × 10 ≈ 7.2 responses per year

These values are close to what the STEPS National simulation tab will show for the same configuration.

Example 8b: Comparing Advanced and Intermediate workshop scenarios

For workshop discussions, users can compare the Advanced scenario above with an Intermediate scenario that uses the Intermediate costs and benefits from Examples 5b and 6b:

  • Intermediate economic cost per cohort: 60,000,000 INR.
  • Intermediate benefit per cohort: 70,800,000 INR.
  • Advanced economic cost per cohort: 45,000,000 INR (at 75,000 INR per trainee per month).
  • Advanced benefit per cohort: 153,418,000 INR.

This highlights how the combination of preference based endorsement, programme cost and epidemiological multipliers can favour different tiers under different assumptions. In the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab these scenarios would appear as rows with their costs, benefits and benefit cost ratios side by side.

9. Tab by tab explanation and replication guide

This section describes each tab in the STEPS interface, the inputs it uses, the calculations it relies on, and the outputs it presents. The aim is to allow analysts to replicate the tool in another environment or to audit each step of the calculation chain.

9.1 Introduction tab

The Introduction tab provides:

  • A narrative overview of FETP tiers and the motivation for the STEPS tool.
  • A short summary of the DCE, cost templates and epidemiological multipliers.
  • High level descriptions of endorsement, cost and benefit outputs.

Text is static and does not depend on user inputs. It reflects the methods described in Sections 1 to 3 of this appendix and can be adapted when the tool is calibrated to other countries or updated DCEs.

9.2 Attributes tab

The Attributes tab lists each DCE attribute and its levels. It shows:

  • The plain language label used in the survey (for example "Mentorship intensity").
  • The levels (for example low, medium, high).
  • The reference level used in the econometric model (coefficient equal to zero).
  • The corresponding variable name in the model (for example MentorHigh).

This tab is a transparent mapping between survey descriptions, programme language and model coding. It supports replication by clarifying exactly how the utility function in Section 2 is constructed from survey design elements.

9.3 Configuration tab

The Configuration tab is the main input tab. For each configuration, the following inputs are set:

  • Tier selection (Frontline, Intermediate, Advanced) mapped to tier dummies.
  • Career incentive choice mapped to incentive dummies.
  • Mentorship intensity mapped to mentorship dummies.
  • Delivery mode mapped to delivery dummies.
  • Expected response time mapped to response time dummies.
  • Cost per trainee per month (INR), which is divided by 1,000 to create the cost variable in the model.
  • Cohort size and number of cohorts.
  • Preference model choice (mixed logit mean or latent class supportive group).
  • Opportunity cost toggle (on or off) and choice of cost template.

When the user changes any of these inputs, the tool:

  1. Recomputes utility and endorsement using Section 2 and Section 3 formulas.
  2. Recomputes programme and economic costs using Section 5 formulas.
  3. Recomputes epidemiological benefits using Section 6 formulas.
  4. Updates national results using Section 7 formulas.

9.4 Results tab

The Results tab displays the main indicators for the current configuration:

  • Endorsement and opt out percentage from Section 3.
  • Programme cost per cohort and economic cost per cohort from Section 5.
  • Epidemiological benefits per cohort from Section 6.
  • Net benefit and benefit cost ratio per cohort from Section 7.
  • Indicative WTP based benefits and WTP summary values from Section 4.

Conceptually, each result item corresponds to one of the formulas already listed. For example:

  • Endorsement is P(endorse) from Section 3.
  • Per cohort cost is C_prog or C_economic from Section 5, depending on the opportunity cost toggle.
  • Per cohort benefit is totalBenefit_per_cohort from Section 6.
  • Benefit cost ratio is BCR_per_cohort as in Section 7.3.

9.5 Costing details tab

The Costing details tab reports:

  • C_prog and, if applicable, C_economic.
  • Component wise breakdown derived as share_k × C_prog for each category k.
  • Separate reporting of opportunity cost when this is included.

This tab closely follows Section 5.3 and Section 5.4. It is particularly useful when analysts want to align STEPS with detailed costing exercises and to check that the assumed shares are reasonable.

9.6 National simulation tab

The National simulation tab uses:

  • Economic cost per cohort C_economic.
  • Benefit per cohort benefit_per_cohort.
  • Graduates per cohort and outbreak responses per year per cohort.
  • Number of cohorts N_cohorts.

It reports:

  • Total national cost, benefit and net benefit from Section 7.1.
  • National graduates and outbreak responses from Section 7.2.
  • National benefit cost ratio, equal to the per cohort ratio.

In a replication setting, analysts can recreate this tab by multiplying all per cohort quantities by the number of cohorts and recomputing ratios accordingly.

9.7 Saved scenarios tab

The Saved scenarios tab stores a snapshot of each configuration the user chooses to save. For each scenario, the following fields are stored conceptually:

  • Scenario label and date.
  • Tier, career incentive, mentorship level, delivery mode and response time.
  • Cost per trainee per month, cohort size and number of cohorts.
  • Endorsement and opt out percentages.
  • Cohort cost, benefits, net benefit and benefit cost ratio.
  • National totals for cost, benefits and epidemiological outputs.
  • Where implemented, WTP based benefits and endorsement weighted WTP benefits.

These records can be exported as JSON, CSV or Excel files so that users can perform additional analyses, such as incremental cost effectiveness comparisons between saved scenarios.

9.8 Advanced settings, Sensitivity / DCE benefits and About STEPS tabs

The Advanced settings tab is described in Section 11. It controls epidemiological and valuation parameters. The Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab is described in detail in Section 10. It displays for each scenario a set of columns that combine cost, WTP benefits, epidemiological benefits and endorsement weighting.

The About STEPS tab provides:

  • A short description of the DCE methods with references to the literature in Section 13.
  • A description of the cost and epidemiological modules.
  • A link to this technical appendix, implemented as an HTML link with target="_blank" so that the appendix opens in a separate browser tab.

Together, these tabs help users understand where the numbers come from and how they can be adapted.

10. Sensitivity, DCE based benefits and endorsement weighting

The premium version of STEPS includes a Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab that summarises, for each scenario, how costs, WTP based benefits, epidemiological benefits and endorsement interact. This section defines the formulas and the column structure used in that tab so that analysts can reproduce it in Excel or other software.

10.1 Definitions for each scenario

For each scenario s the following quantities are computed:

  • C_s: economic cost per cohort (INR), from Section 5.
  • B_s_epi: epidemiological benefit per cohort (INR), from Section 6.
  • B_s_WTP,overall: WTP based benefit per cohort (INR) using the mixed logit model, from Section 4.3.
  • B_s_WTP,response: WTP component per cohort associated with outbreak response time, from Section 4.4.
  • B_s_WTP,class2: WTP based benefit per cohort (INR) using the supportive latent class parameters.
  • endorsement_s,overall: endorsement rate from the mixed logit model (Section 3).
  • endorsement_s,class2: endorsement rate from the supportive latent class.

Effective WTP based benefits are:

B\_s,WTP,effective,overall = B\_s\_WTP,overall × endorsement\_s,overall B\_s,WTP,effective,class2 = B\_s\_WTP,class2 × endorsement\_s,class2

Combined benefits that add epidemiological and WTP components can be defined as:

B\_s,total,overall = B\_s\_epi + B\_s\_WTP,overall B\_s,total,class2 = B\_s\_epi + B\_s\_WTP,class2

10.2 Column structure in the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab

Each row of the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab represents one scenario and includes at least the following columns:

Column Label Formula or definition
1 Scenario User defined scenario name.
2 Tier / design Text summary of tier, mentorship, delivery, response time and career incentive.
3 Cost (INR) C_s, economic cost per cohort.
4 Total WTP benefit (overall) B_s_WTP,overall.
5 WTP from outbreak response capacity B_s_WTP,response.
6 Epi based outbreak benefit B_s_epi (zero if epi benefits are not activated).
7 Endorsement (overall) endorsement_s,overall.
8 Effective WTP benefit (overall) B_s,WTP,effective,overall = B_s_WTP,overall × endorsement_s,overall.
9 BCR (epi only) B_s_epi / C_s.
10 BCR (DCE WTP only) B_s_WTP,overall / C_s.
11 BCR (combined epi + WTP) B_s,total,overall / C_s.
12 NPV (epi only) B_s_epi - C_s.
13 NPV (DCE WTP only) B_s_WTP,overall - C_s.
14 NPV (combined epi + WTP) B_s,total,overall - C_s.
15 Total WTP benefit (supportive class) B_s_WTP,class2.
16 Endorsement (supportive class) endorsement_s,class2.
17 Effective WTP benefit (supportive class) B_s,WTP,effective,class2.

Additional columns can be added to store incremental costs and benefits relative to a reference scenario (for example the current FETP portfolio) using the incremental formulas in Section 7.4.

10.3 Class based sensitivity

The latent class model allows separate reporting of benefits and endorsement for supportive and more sceptical groups. In practice, the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab can be used in at least three ways:

  • Overall perspective: use mixed logit averages for endorsement and WTP and focus on columns 3 to 14.
  • Supportive perspective: focus on columns 15 to 17, which reflect a more favourable stakeholder group.
  • Conservative perspective: if class specific results for the more sceptical class are added, these can be used to construct lower bound scenarios.

This structure makes it clear how benefits change when the analysis is restricted to more or less supportive stakeholder groups, without changing any cost inputs.

11. Advanced settings

The Advanced settings tab exposes key epidemiological and display parameters so that technical users can align STEPS with national assumptions without editing code. The main adjustable parameters are:

  • Graduation rates for each tier.
  • Outbreak responses per cohort per year for each tier.
  • Value per graduate for each tier.
  • Value per outbreak response for each tier.
  • Planning horizon in years.
  • Display exchange rate (INR per USD).
  • Where implemented, response time multipliers m_15 and m_7.

Changing any of these parameters immediately updates:

  • Per cohort epidemiological metrics and monetary benefits.
  • National simulation results.
  • Benefit cost ratio and net benefit in the Results and National simulation tabs.
  • Columns that depend on epidemiological benefits in the Sensitivity / DCE benefits tab.

For stability, the tool does not change the underlying preference coefficients when advanced settings are edited. Only the translation between graduates, outbreak responses and monetary benefits is affected.

Example 9: Increasing the value per outbreak response

Suppose a new analysis suggests that each outbreak response avoided has a value of 50,000,000 INR instead of 40,000,000 INR. If the user updates the value per outbreak response for the relevant tier:

  • Graduate benefits remain unchanged.
  • Outbreak related benefits increase by 25 percent.
  • Total benefits and net benefits increase.
  • The benefit cost ratio increases proportionally to the change in benefits.

This allows country teams to align STEPS with updated numbers from health security or burden of disease models while keeping the preference based endorsement structure intact.

Example 9b: Adjusting graduation rates after programme strengthening

If monitoring data suggest that graduation rates for Intermediate programmes have increased from 0.90 to 0.95 after curriculum strengthening:

  • Update the Intermediate graduation rate in the Advanced settings from 0.90 to 0.95.
  • Graduates per cohort increase proportionally, raising graduate related benefits.
  • Outbreak related benefits remain unchanged unless outbreak multipliers are also updated.
  • Overall benefit cost ratios improve slightly for Intermediate configurations.

This provides a simple way to reflect improvements in programme quality in the STEPS outputs.

The epi_config.json file provides the same parameters in a structured format that can be edited before deployment. When present, the tool reads this file on load and uses its contents as defaults. If the file is not present, built in default values are used.

12. Recommended use and limits

STEPS is designed as a transparent decision aid for use with ministries, partners and funders such as the World Bank, WHO and CDC. It is best used to:

  • Compare alternative FETP programme designs and identify high value options.
  • Support business case documents with credible endorsement, cost and benefit metrics.
  • Structure workshop discussions around trade offs between cost, endorsement and epidemiological impact.
  • Generate WTP based benefit indices that reflect stakeholder preferences for programme features.

It is not intended to replace:

  • Detailed epidemiological models of disease transmission.
  • Full economic evaluations that include discounting of costs and health outcomes over long horizons.
  • Comprehensive fiscal planning tools for multi programme portfolios.
In practice, STEPS works best when used alongside detailed modelling. STEPS can highlight promising configurations quickly. Detailed models can then be applied to a shortlist of options if required for large scale investment decisions. The WTP based metrics and class specific sensitivity analyses provide an additional lens on stakeholder support for different FETP scale up strategies.
