1. Introduction and purpose of the STEPS tool

The STEPS FETP Scale up Decision Aid is a scenario based economic and preference informed tool designed to support planning decisions for Field Epidemiology Training Programs (FETP) in India. It combines (i) stated preference evidence on programme design features from a discrete choice experiment, (ii) programme cost construction using observed cost structures from WHO, NIE and NCDC programmes, and (iii) epidemiological benefit monetisation using literature calibrated outbreak cost savings. The tool produces transparent outputs intended for policy appraisal, comparison of programme configurations, and structured reporting.

2. Overview of the preference study and mixed logit model

The tool uses outputs from a discrete choice experiment (DCE) to quantify preferences for alternative FETP programme designs. The DCE is analysed using a mixed logit model (also called random parameters logit). The mixed logit supports preference heterogeneity across respondents and produces choice probability predictions for any programme profile within the attribute range used in the experiment.

Random utility structure

Ui j t = Vi j t + εi j t
Utility U for respondent i choosing alternative j in task t is the sum of a systematic component V and an unobserved component ε. The tool uses V to compute predicted choice probabilities and welfare measures such as willingness to pay.
Vi j t = ASCj + β′ xj t + βc · Costj t
The systematic utility includes an alternative specific constant (ASC) capturing baseline preference for implementing a training programme relative to opt out, a vector of attribute indicators x (programme type, mentoring, delivery mode, and career incentive), and a cost term. βc is typically negative, meaning higher costs reduce utility.

Mixed logit integration over heterogeneous preferences

Pi j = ∫ exp(Vi j(β)) / Σk exp(Vi k(β)) · f(β | θ) dβ
In mixed logit, β varies across respondents according to a distribution f(β | θ). The tool uses the estimated distribution parameters θ (for example means and standard deviations) to compute choice probabilities by simulation, averaging over draws of β.

Simulation within the tool

The tool treats each scenario as a programme profile defined by the selected attribute levels and cost. For that profile, the tool computes predicted probability of being chosen relative to an opt out alternative. The predicted probability is used for two purposes: (i) reporting the implied acceptability or endorsement of the programme, and (ii) applying an endorsement adjustment to WTP in sensitivity analysis when the analyst prefers conservative welfare aggregation.

Coefficient transparency. The model coefficients used by the tool are fixed inputs estimated from the preference study. The tool stores these coefficients internally and applies them exactly as described above. If a coefficient set is updated, the calculations in Sections 8 and 14 remain unchanged, but numerical WTP results will update mechanically.

Attributes used for scenario definition

Fixed response capacity explanation: response capacity is fixed at 7 days

Why response capacity is fixed at 7 days in scenario inputs. Response capacity is treated as an outcome of training and system performance, not as a user controlled programme design choice. Fixing response capacity at 7 days avoids double counting benefits because outbreak savings are already constructed as an output of training (Section 9). It also enforces a clean separation between inputs (what planners can select and fund) and outputs (what the system achieves after implementing the training and associated support).

In the tool interface, the response capacity field can remain visible for transparency. Options other than 7 days are intentionally inactive to prevent analysts from mixing an outcome measure into the design choice set.

4. Cost construction methodology

The core user facing cost input is the programme cost per trainee per month, expressed in Indian rupees. This corresponds to the cost attribute used in the DCE and is therefore constrained to remain within the experimental range to avoid extrapolation beyond the preference data.

Unit conversions

Cost per trainee for full programme (₹) = Cost per trainee per month (₹) × Programme duration (months)
The tool multiplies the monthly cost by the number of months in the selected programme type to obtain the per trainee cost for completing the full programme.
Direct programme cost per cohort (₹) = Cost per trainee for full programme (₹) × Cohort size (trainees)
This is the direct financial resource requirement implied by the scenario cost level for one cohort, before adding indirect or opportunity costs.

The tool treats programme duration as programme specific (for example 3, 12, and 24 months). The cohort size can be taken from observed programme structures (Section 5) or configured for simulation when the analyst is scaling up (Section 12).

How cost shares are calculated and applied

Direct total (₹) = Grand total (₹) − Indirect costs (₹)
For each programme type, the tool defines direct totals as the residual after subtracting indirect costs from the grand total in the cost dataset.
Share of component c = DirectCostc / Direct total
Each direct cost component is converted into a share by dividing it by the direct total for that programme type.
Scaled component cost in scenario (₹) = Share of component c × Scenario direct cost per cohort (₹)
The scenario’s direct cost per cohort, implied by the user selected cost level, is allocated across components using the shares. This preserves the observed cost structure while matching the scenario’s total direct cost level.

6. Program cost per trainee and cohort calculations

This section documents the cost calculations reported on the Results tab. The tool reports both direct programme cost and total economic cost, depending on whether opportunity costs (indirect costs) are included.

Direct programme costs

Ctrainee = cmonth × M
Ctrainee is direct cost per trainee for the full programme. cmonth is cost per trainee per month and M is duration in months.
Ccohort, direct = Ctrainee × N
Ccohort, direct is direct cost for one cohort. N is cohort size.

Economic costs with indirect costs

Indirect ratio ρ = Indirect costs / Direct total
ρ is computed from the WHO, NIE and NCDC totals for each programme type and used to scale the scenario’s direct cost into an economic cost that includes opportunity costs.
Ccohort, economic = Ccohort, direct × (1 + ρ)
When opportunity costs are included, the tool multiplies direct cohort cost by (1 + ρ). When opportunity costs are excluded, the tool sets Ccohort, economic = Ccohort, direct.

7. Opportunity cost inclusion and exclusion logic

Opportunity costs and indirect costs can be material in training programmes, particularly where staff time, shared facilities, or other non budgetary resources are required. The tool therefore supports two reporting modes.

Mode A: Include opportunity costs. The tool reports Total economic cost using Ccohort, economic = Ccohort, direct × (1 + ρ), where ρ is the indirect ratio for the programme type. This mode is appropriate for economic evaluation and full social resource accounting.

Mode B: Exclude opportunity costs. The tool reports costs using only the direct cohort cost Ccohort, direct. This mode is appropriate for budget impact analysis, financing discussions, and when opportunity costs are accounted for elsewhere in the decision process.

Switching the toggle changes only the cost denominator used in BCR and net benefit computations. All preference based outputs and outbreak benefit outputs remain unchanged because they are constructed independently of the opportunity cost toggle.

8. Willingness to pay calculations

The tool computes willingness to pay (WTP) from the mixed logit model as a marginal rate of substitution between a change in programme attributes and the cost attribute. WTP is reported as an amount per trainee per month, and can be aggregated to the cohort and planning horizon scale for reporting.

Core WTP definition

WTP (₹ per trainee per month) = − (ΔV / βc)
ΔV is the change in systematic utility between a scenario programme and the comparator (typically opt out). βc is the cost coefficient in utility per rupee. The minus sign converts the negative marginal utility of cost into a positive WTP value.
ΔV = (ASC + Σ attribute coefficients for selected levels) − (ASC and attributes for opt out)
When opt out is the comparator, the opt out alternative typically has zero attribute indicators and a separate constant. The tool uses the estimated model specification embedded in its coefficient set.

Total WTP aggregation for a cohort

Total WTP for cohort (₹) = WTP (₹ per trainee per month) × Duration (months) × Cohort size
This aggregation converts monthly per trainee WTP into a total WTP over the full programme for all trainees in a cohort.

Endorsement adjusted WTP (optional sensitivity)

In some reporting contexts, analysts prefer to discount welfare measures by the probability that the programme is endorsed or chosen. The tool therefore provides an endorsement adjusted WTP option in sensitivity analysis.

Endorsement adjusted total WTP (₹) = Total WTP (₹) × P(endorsement)
P(endorsement) is the mixed logit predicted probability that the scenario programme is chosen relative to the opt out alternative. This adjustment is conservative because it scales down welfare when acceptability is low.

Interpretation boundary. WTP is a preference based measure derived from a stated preference study. It reflects trade offs respondents make in the experiment and should be interpreted as an economic value metric within the attribute and cost ranges tested in the DCE.

9. Epidemiological benefit construction

The tool monetises epidemiological benefits as expected cost savings from averting or substantially reducing large outbreaks, expressed in rupees. The tool’s outbreak benefit module is designed to be transparent and sensitivity driven because outbreak costs and the effect of training on outbreak outcomes are uncertain and context dependent.

Expected outbreak savings: general structure

Annual outbreak savings (₹ per year) = p × V
p is the annual probability that a cohort, together with the strengthened system it supports, prevents or materially reduces a large outbreak that would otherwise impose cost V. V is the value per outbreak (rupees) selected in Settings.
Present value of outbreak savings (₹) = Σt=1..H [ Annual outbreak savings / (1 + r)t ]
The tool discounts future outbreak savings over a planning horizon H (years) at discount rate r. Discounting ensures that benefits occurring further in the future contribute less to present value than immediate benefits.

Why response capacity is not treated as an input

Because response capacity is an outcome of training and system performance, it is fixed at 7 days in scenarios (Section 3). The outbreak benefit module already captures how training translates into outbreak cost savings through the probability parameter p and the value per outbreak V. Allowing response capacity to be varied as a design input would risk double counting by treating the same outcome both as a direct scenario input and as a monetised benefit output.

The STEPS baseline value per outbreak is set to ₹20.0 billion and sensitivity analysis uses ₹8.3 billion and ₹166 billion. The literature above provides order of magnitude context for these values. The tool also provides additional smaller outbreak values (for example ₹40 million, ₹80 million) for analysts who wish to explore conservative assumptions for more localised outbreaks.

Why ₹20 billion is used as the baseline and how sensitivity values map to real world outbreaks

The baseline value of ₹20.0 billion is intended to represent a conservative central estimate for the economic cost that could plausibly be avoided or substantially reduced by improved detection and response during a large multi state outbreak in India. It is materially below the macroeconomic losses reported for severe epidemics such as Ebola and SARS, while remaining large enough to reflect disruption beyond direct health sector costs, consistent with the magnitude of national burden estimates reported for dengue and other major outbreaks in the region.

The lower bound value of ₹8.3 billion corresponds to US$0.1 billion at ₹83 per US$, representing sizeable outbreaks where costs plausibly fall in the single digit billions of rupees, including major localised outbreaks or less severe national events. The upper bound value of ₹166 billion corresponds to US$2.0 billion, representing rare high impact events that plausibly involve major macroeconomic disruption. Sensitivity analysis uses these values to show how benefit cost conclusions change when outbreak costs are assumed to be lower or higher than the baseline.

11. Benefit cost ratios and net present value

The Results and Sensitivity tabs report net benefit, benefit cost ratio (BCR), and net present value (NPV). These metrics combine cost and benefit modules described earlier.

Net benefit

Net benefit (₹) = Total WTP (₹) + PV(outbreak savings) (₹) − Total economic cost (₹)
Net benefit is positive when the combined monetised benefits exceed costs under the selected assumptions.

Benefit cost ratio

BCR = [ Total WTP (₹) + PV(outbreak savings) (₹) ] / Total economic cost (₹)
BCR above 1 indicates monetised benefits exceed costs. When opportunity costs are excluded, the denominator is direct cost rather than economic cost (Section 7).

Discounting and present value

PV factor for annual flow over H years at rate r = (1 − (1 + r)−H) / r
When annual outbreak savings are assumed constant, the tool applies the annuity present value factor to compute PV(outbreak savings). Discounting is standard in economic appraisal to reflect time preference and opportunity cost of capital; the exact choice of r is policy specific and is therefore configurable. A general reference for discounting practice in economic evaluation is Drummond et al., Methods for the Economic Evaluation of Health Care Programmes (Oxford University Press).

12. National simulation scaling

The tool supports scaling from a single cohort scenario to a national planning simulation. The scaling logic is deterministic and based on the configuration parameters selected by the analyst.

Scale up quantities

Number of trainees over horizon = Trainees per cohort × Cohorts per year × H
This converts cohort level scale into total trainees trained over the planning horizon H. If cohorts are entered as a total number rather than per year, the tool uses that total directly and does not multiply by H.

Scaling direct costs

Total direct cost over horizon (₹) = Ccohort, direct × Number of cohorts implemented
Costs scale linearly with the number of cohorts, holding cost per trainee per month and duration fixed for a given programme type.

Scaling outbreak savings

Total PV(outbreak savings) for scale up (₹) = Σ over cohorts [ PV(outbreak savings per cohort) ]
If cohorts are implemented at different times, the tool can discount each cohort’s benefit stream from its start year. In the simplest configuration, all cohorts are treated as implemented at the start of the horizon and benefits accrue over H years.

When analysts need more granular timing, they should use the Settings and Configuration controls to match the expected roll out schedule, then rely on the same present value formulas in Section 11.

13. Sensitivity analysis framework

Sensitivity analysis in the tool recomputes results under alternative assumptions while holding the scenario programme design constant. The tool reports sensitivity tables so that decision makers can see whether conclusions are robust to uncertainty in outbreak values, endorsement adjustments, and discounting.

Parameters varied in sensitivity analysis

Sensitivity dimension Values explored What changes in calculations
Benefit definition options WTP only; outbreak savings only; combined Net benefit and BCR are recomputed using the selected benefit components, enabling analysts to separate preference based value from epidemiological savings.
Endorsement adjusted WTP Off or on When on, Total WTP is multiplied by the predicted endorsement probability from the mixed logit model (Section 8).
Outbreak value sensitivity Baseline ₹20.0 billion; lower ₹8.3 billion; upper ₹166 billion; additional smaller values such as ₹40 million and ₹80 million PV(outbreak savings) is recomputed using the alternative value per outbreak while holding the annual probability parameter p fixed.
Planning horizon and discounting User selected H and r PV factors and any present value outputs update mechanically using the discounting equations in Section 11.

Interpretation of sensitivity tables

Sensitivity tables should be read as conditional statements of the form: if outbreak values are low, or if endorsement is low, or if discounting is high, then the implied net benefit and BCR change accordingly. When BCR remains above 1 across the plausible range, the policy conclusion is more robust. When BCR crosses 1 within plausible ranges, the decision is sensitive and should be supported by additional evidence on outbreak costs or programme effectiveness.

15. Copilot interpretation and JSON export

The Copilot tab provides a structured workflow for generating policy ready narrative outputs. The tool generates (i) a human readable brief that summarises the selected scenario and key results, and (ii) a JSON object that encodes all assumptions, inputs, and outputs so that a language model can draft a consistent policy brief without missing key parameters.

What text is generated

The tool generates text that explains the scenario in plain language, reports the core cost and benefit outputs, and highlights key sensitivity results. The narrative is designed to be pasted into Microsoft Copilot or another drafting environment. The narrative is descriptive, not prescriptive. It does not make funding decisions; it reports quantified outputs conditional on assumptions.

What JSON fields are included

JSON field group Examples of fields Purpose
Scenario definition programType, mentoring, deliveryMode, careerIncentive, costPerMonth, durationMonths, responseCapacityDays Ensures the drafted brief states the scenario correctly and consistently.
Scale inputs cohortSize, numberOfCohorts, planningHorizonYears Supports national scaling and total budget projections.
Cost assumptions includeOpportunityCost, indirectRatio, costSharesByCategory Documents economic cost accounting and cost breakdown logic.
Preference model outputs wtpPerMonth, totalWtp, endorsementProbability, endorsementAdjustedWtp Ensures preference based results are reproduced faithfully in narrative.

Outcome metrics epidemiologicalBenefitPV, netBenefit, bcr, npv Provides the headline metrics for policy appraisal.

What Copilot does not do

Copilot outputs do not validate the underlying evidence, do not replace stakeholder consultation, and do not provide epidemiological forecasting beyond the explicit outbreak value and probability assumptions encoded in the tool. Copilot is a drafting aid for communication, not a decision rule.

16. Summary of assumptions and limitations

Key assumptions encoded in the tool

Assumption How the tool implements it Implication
Programme design is represented by a fixed set of attributes and levels Scenarios are defined by programme type, mentoring, delivery mode, career incentive, and cost per trainee per month. Outputs are valid for comparisons within the tested design space; additional features outside the attribute set require new evidence.
Cost slider remains within the DCE range The cost input is constrained to the range used in the preference study. Prevents extrapolation beyond the preference data but may restrict exploration of extreme programme costs.
Response capacity fixed at 7 days Response capacity is treated as an outcome and not a user controlled input. Avoids double counting and supports clean separation between programme design choices and outcome benefits.
Cost structure shares are stable under scaling Component shares from WHO, NIE and NCDC data are applied proportionally to scenario direct costs. Maintains realism in cost breakdown, but may not capture nonlinear economies of scale or context specific input prices.
Outbreak savings are represented by a value per outbreak and an effectiveness probability Outbreak values are calibrated using literature context and varied in sensitivity analysis; effectiveness is represented transparently as a parameter in benefit construction. Supports transparent appraisal under uncertainty; conclusions can be sensitive to outbreak value and effectiveness assumptions.
Discounting and planning horizon are configurable Present value calculations apply discount rate r over horizon H. Longer horizons and lower discount rates increase present value of future outbreak savings.

Limitations to consider when defending results

Preference based WTP reflects stated choices within an experimental context and can be sensitive to framing, sample composition, and attribute specification. Cost scaling uses proportional shares and therefore abstracts from local price variation and scale economies. Outbreak cost savings are highly uncertain and context dependent; the tool addresses this by making outbreak value selection explicit and by encouraging sensitivity analysis across lower and higher plausible values anchored in the calibration table.

Reference note. The outbreak value evidence base and the conversion logic are documented in Section 10. General methods for economic evaluation and discounting are discussed in standard texts such as Drummond et al., Methods for the Economic Evaluation of Health Care Programmes (Oxford University Press). The tool’s primary empirical inputs are the estimated mixed logit coefficients from the preference study and the WHO, NIE and NCDC cost dataset presented in Section 5.
